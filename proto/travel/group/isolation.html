Mandatory isolation and quarantine

Depending on your situtation, you may need to follow a mandatory isolation or a mandatory quarantine period when entering Canada.

Your quarantine or isolation plan

Upon entry to Canada, you are required to acknowledge that you must:

  • isolate for 14 days if you have symptoms of COVID-19 or
  • quarantine (self-isolate) for 14 days if you do not have symptoms

You will be asked if you have a suitable place to isolate or quarantine (self-isolate), where you will have basic necessities, such as food and medication, and where you will not have contact with vulnerable people.

The 14-day period starts from the date you arrive in Canada. You may be subject to additional provincial or territorial quarantine time at your final destination.

If you do not have a quarantine or isolation plan

Foreign nationals without a quarantine plan may be denied entry in Canada.

If you do not have an adequate place to quarantine, the Chief Public Health Officer of Canada will designate a facility where you must remain for 14 days.

Without symptoms: Mandatory quarantine

First, you should determine whether or not you can come to Canada.

If you can come to Canada and you have no symptoms, you must quarantine (self-isolate) for 14 days. You are at risk of developing symptoms and infecting others. This is mandatory.

You must also follow any provincial or territorial requirements, depending on your destination.

With symptoms: Mandatory isolation

Foreign nationals with symptoms will not be allowed into Canada. Only Canadian citizens, permanent residents, persons registered under the Indian Act, and protected persons may enter Canada with symptoms. You will not be able to board a flight and enter by air if you have symptoms.

You must isolate for 14 days. This is mandatory.

You must also follow any provincial or territorial requirements, depending on your destination.

Who is exempt from quarantine

Only people who provide essential services, maintain the flow of essentials goods or people, and those who must regularly cross the border as part of an integrated trans-border community, or are recieving medical care (non-related to COVID-19) are exempt from the quarantine requirements.

If your reason for entering Canada falls undoubtedly under an exemption in the list, you do not need to apply for a letter of exemption. You may have to follow certain provincial and territorial restrictions, depending on your destination.

Medical and health care

  • a person who enters Canada for the purpose of receiving essential medical services or treatments, other than services or treatments related to COVID-19
  • a person returning to Canada after seeking medical treatment in another country, other than services or treatments related to COVID-19 mentioned in meeting, but can't find the Canada.ca content wording/confirmation
  • a person permitted to work in Canada as a student in a health field under paragraph 186(p) of the Immigration and Refugee Protection Regulations, as long as they do not directly care for persons 65 years of age or older within the 14-day period that begins on the day on which the person enters Canada
  • a person who enters Canada for the purpose of providing medical care, transporting essential medical equipment, supplies or means of treatment, or delivering, maintaining or repairing medically-necessary equipment or devices, as long as they do not directly care for persons 65 years of age or older within the 14-day period that begins on the day on which the person enters Canada
  • a person entering Canada from the U.S. as a non-healthcare professional to care for a family member considered vulnerable mentioned in meeting, but can't find the IRCC content wording/confirmation

Essential work

  • a person or any person in a class of persons whom the Chief Public Health Officer determines will provide an essential service; such as truck drivers or marine transport crew who are moving essential goods
  • a crew member as defined in subsection 101.01(1) of the Canadian Aviation Regulations or a person who enters Canada only to become such a crew member
  • a member of a crew as defined in subsection 3(1) of the Immigration and Refugee Protection Regulations or a person who enters Canada only to become such a crew member
  • a member of the Canadian Forces or a visiting force as defined in section 2 of the Visiting Forces Act
  • a person, including a captain, deckhand, observer, inspector, scientist and any other person supporting commercial or research fishing-related activities, who enters Canada aboard a Canadian fishing vessel or a foreign fishing vessel as defined in subsection 2(1) of the Coastal Fisheries Protection Act, for the purpose of carrying out fishing or fishing-related activities, including offloading of fish, repairs, provisioning the vessel and exchange of crew

Trans-border communities or everyday functions

  • a person who enters Canada within the boundaries of an integrated trans-border community that exists on both sides of the Canada-United States border and who is a habitual resident of that community, if entering Canada is necessary for carrying out an everyday function within that community; such as buying groceries or gas when the community access is in Canada
  • a person who enters Canada if the entry is necessary to return to their habitual place of residence in Canada after carrying out an everyday function that, due to geographical constraints, must involve entering the United States; such as returning from work as an emergency responder or doctor employed in the U.S.

Other special circumstances

  • a person who enters Canada at the invitation of the Minister of Health for the purpose of assisting in the COVID-19 response
  • a person or any person in a class of persons whose presence in Canada is determined by the Minister of Foreign Affairs, the Minister of Citizenship and Immigration or the Minister of Public Safety and Emergency Preparedness to be in the national interest, as long as the person complies with any conditions imposed on them by the relevant Minister to minimize the risk of introduction or spread of COVID-19
  • a person of national interest, as long as the person complies with any conditions imposed on them by the relevant Minister to minimize the risk of introduction or spread of COVID-19

Request a quarantine exemption

Although you may have a compassionate and compelling reason, you cannot be exempted from a 14 day quarantine for:

  • a visit to a sick or dying relative
  • a funeral
  • non-essential work
  • non-essential reasons

If you believe you are entering Canada for another essential reason not outline in the Emergency Order and you cannot plan for a 14 day quarantine, you will require an exemption.

How to request a quarantine exemption

To complete a quarantine exemption request, email us at: phac.emergencyorder-decretdurgencecovid19.aspc@canada.ca.

To support your request you must include the following information, at minimum:

  • number of travellers and country of citizenship of each traveller
  • planned travel dates
  • specific purpose for travel to Canada
  • an explanation as to why you are of the view that you qualify for exemption and are unable to plan for a 14 day quarantine

Please note that the Canada Border Services Agency is responsible for the administration of Emergency Orders made under the Quarantine Act at Canada's points of entry. As such, entry to Canada (and subsequent public health measures) is permitted at the discretion of a Border Services Officer who is required to weigh the evidence present at the time of entry against reasonableness and the overall risks to public health.

Complying with the emergency order

The Government of Canada has put in place an Emergency Order under the Quarantine Act. It applies to all travellers arriving in Canada. Its purpose is to slow the spread of COVID-19 in Canada.

Failure to comply with this order is an offence under the Quarantine Act and could lead to imprisonment and/or fines.

How to comply when you arrive in Canada

If you are permitted to enter Canada, you will be:

  • asked if you have a cough, fever or difficulty breathing
  • required to acknowledge that you must:
    • isolate for 14 days if you have symptoms of COVID-19 or
    • quarantine (self-isolate) for 14 days if you do not have symptoms
  • asked if you have a suitable place to isolate or quarantine (self-isolate)
    • a suitable place is one where you will have basic necessities, such as food and medication, and where you will not have contact with vulnerable people
  • given instructions about your obligations under the Emergency Order

The ArriveCAN mobile application (iOS, Android, or web format):

  • enables travellers to validate their 14-day quarantine or isolation plan after they arrive in Canada
  • facilitates compliance with the Quarantine Act

Violating any instructions provided to you when you entered Canada is an offence under the Quarantine Act and could lead to up to:

  • 6 months in prison and/or
  • $750,000 in fines

Further, a person who causes a risk of imminent death or serious bodily harm to another person while wilfully or recklessly contravening this act or the regulations could be liable for:

  • a fine of up to $1,000,000 or
  • imprisonment of up to 3 years or
  • both

The Contraventions Act has been changed to give police (including RCMP, provincial and local police) more power to enforce the Quarantine Act. They can now issue tickets to people who do not comply with the act. Fines range from $275 to $1000.

To better understand the roles and responsibilities of federal, provincial and territorial governments and law enforcement agencies with compliance and enforcement, see Compliance and enforcement of the Quarantine Act
